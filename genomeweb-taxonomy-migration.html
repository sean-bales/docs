GenomeWeb migrates to Arc on a defined tag vocabulary. Sponsor and reader expectations intact.

01The claim

The migration settles the tag vocabulary in Arc. Downstream systems get a cleaner input.

A curated section map and a closed tag vocabulary. New tag creation runs through admin approval. The Drupal long-tail is dropped.

Scope. The taxonomy direction is set. The sponsor operating model is set: Topic Takeover moves to an editorial newsletter with exclusive sponsorship — theme-level standing infrastructure, edited per send, sponsor-facing experience unchanged. See A3 and A4. The reader-side UX (where section follows live, how the Preference Center shapes) is a downstream product decision that this deck does not resolve.

Sources: GNW taxonomy decision doc (2026-04-21); six-year Drupal content audit (23,384 articles / 2,105 applied tags / 141,534 tag assignments); executive editor’s site-structure and TT, My Topics, and 2025 engagement curation; Sailthru segment rules and Content Library; GAM line-item register. Pulled 04/2026.

02Sponsor process

Cutover tightens the vocabulary. Topic Takeover moves to an editorial newsletter with exclusive sponsorship.

Sponsor experience unchanged. Operator work shifts from custom build per campaign to standing theme infrastructure edited per send.

Current
  • Free-text content tagging. Editorial tags every article against the accumulated Drupal vocabulary; no mechanical governance.
  • Bespoke campaign assembly. Custom audience list, custom feed, custom template built from scratch for every sponsor.
  • Per-send hand-built newsletters. Marketing automation carves audiences and clones templates per send.
  • Hand-typed competitor exclusions. Up to ninety targeting-clause edits across the live book per new launch; one sponsor excludes sixty-plus historical competitors.
Future
  • Dropdown tagging from the closed vocabulary. New tag creation runs through admin approval. Shipping at cutover.
  • Standing theme infrastructure. One audience cohort and one content feed per top-level theme (Cancer, Diagnostics, Drug Testing, etc.) — built once, reused across sponsors in that theme. Sponsors still pick tags like today.
  • Editorial-edits-per-send model. Each send is a light configuration of the standing theme feed — tune to this sponsor’s tag list, apply competitor exclusions, schedule. No rebuild from zero.
  • Concurrent sponsors per theme. Multiple sponsors can share a theme with feed-level exclusion between them. Exclusive placement per send preserved; structural per-package occupancy isn’t required.
Shipping at cutover: the free-text Drupal vocabulary tightens to a closed, curated Arc vocabulary; new tag creation runs through admin approval; the long-tail drops. The sponsor-side operational shift — custom build per campaign → standing theme infrastructure edited per send — rolls out in the marketing automation workstream tied to cutover. Sponsor-facing product unchanged. See A2 (today) and A3 (future).

Sources: ops audit of marketing automation, ad ops, and sales workflows; GAM line-item exclusion patterns; sponsor campaign register. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: 15 active GenomeWeb sponsor campaigns.

03Reader experience

Readers follow sections, not tags. Tags power advertising and newsletter infrastructure underneath.

Separating the two resolves an ambiguity the current follow list carries.

Current
  • Three opt-in surfaces. Preferences UI, topic-page button, article-page button.
  • Follow does not equal subscribe. Topic follow and newsletter subscription are separate plumbing.
  • Followable list mirrors the full Drupal taxonomy. The accumulated tag list shows up in My Topics, mixing entities (company names, drug names) with concepts (methods, disease areas).
  • Personalization runs through an opaque Sailthru data feed. Tag-matching at render time inside an unauditable Zephyr template.
  • Relevance comes from explicit follows only. Reading behavior never feeds back.
Future
  • One opt-in surface. Preferences UI only; on-site topic and article follow buttons retire.
  • Follow equals subscribe. A single opt-in flow does both.
  • Followable list is the curated section map. Editorial governs the section map; no free-text terms surface to the reader. Tags stay in the infrastructure layer — not reader-facing.
  • Personalization matches reader section follows against editorially-sectioned articles. Same render-time mechanism as today, on a tighter signal.
  • Relevance comes from explicit follows. No inferred reading-signal layer at launch.
Opt-in surfaces 3 → 1. Reader-facing vocabulary tightens from the free-text Drupal list to the curated section map. The tag vocabulary stays in the infrastructure layer (advertising, newsletter segmentation). Observed basis: in a six-month GAM window, 168 concurrent-campaign pairs would have collided on a top-level section alone — the tag layer is what keeps per-sponsor targeting distinct. See A5 and A6 for the lifecycle in detail.

Sources: Sailthru user records and send logs; on-site preferences-UI analytics; Drupal taxonomy export; GAM six-month campaign-concurrency analysis. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: GenomeWeb stated followers (n = 12,891) and active-tier newsletter recipients.

04Architecture spine

Page key-values handle article targeting. The newsletter-click URL handles Topic Takeover attribution.

Arc holds the closed vocabulary. Editors pick from it at publish. Downstream systems consume the page key-values.

  • One vocabulary in Arc. Editorial-governed sections and an admin-approved tag list. Editors pick from it at publish via dropdown.
  • Two bridges to GAM, coexisting. Page key-values for article-page targeting (SOV, TT, 100% SOV); newsletter_campaign URL parameter for Topic Takeover boundary-level (TTBL) targeting. Both preserved; neither replaces the other.
  • One render pattern for the newsletter. Send-time match reads reader section follows against indexed, editorially-tagged articles — same pattern as today, tighter signal.
  • Ring-fenced footprint. GNW-scoped within Arc’s org-global tag namespace. Migrable if the org picks a different direction later.

Architecture reference. See A2–A10 for the system-by-system breakdown of each lane.

Bucket 2 · Sponsor lifecycle

A2Sponsor lifecycle today

Today’s sponsor lifecycle runs through five systems, an external vendor, and ~90 hand-typed exclusion edits per launch.

The Sailthru-to-GAM bridge is a sponsor tag carried in the email URL.

Setup — what gets built once per sponsor
  • Sales hands the sponsor’s tag list and flight dates to marketing automation. The IO signs in the sales system.
  • Elan.Web auto-syncs the GAM forward book. At IO sign, Elan.Web creates 9–27 INACTIVE line items per order across the three ad-unit trees — Topic Takeover Sponsorship / Single Keyword / Multiple Keyword CPM. Status INACTIVE, isMissingCreatives: true, stub targeting on page_type IS (article). Currently 111 such INACTIVE LIs are forward-booked for 2026-06 and later across 8 parent orders. These sit waiting for YourBow to convert them.
  • Marketing automation builds the Sailthru side in two steps: (1) smart-list build — 10–21 tag interests, hand-tuned threshold, ancestor scoping to consented users; (2) archetype template build — Zephyr, from scratch, wired to a sponsor-specific data feed. Plus a client code entered into a GenomeWeb-only in-house tool hosted on S3.
  • YourBow (external vendor) converts the INACTIVE LIs when flights open and traffics Convention-2 delivering LIs — naming convention GNW_<adv>_CID nnnnn_WATRICLE_MLT_{TTBL|TT|100%SOV}_YYYY-MM-DD — in triplicate across the three ad-unit trees (gnwo, gpo, gdxo). ~9 delivering LIs per campaign-month. Ad ops then hand-types competitor exclusions onto each one — one inline list per LI, no central registry.
SOV targeting predicate (for context)
  • Full-SOV delivering LIs carry an OR-of-ANDs predicate: (page_type IS article AND tag IS [brand-tag]) OR (page_type IS category AND page_id IS [taxonomy-NNNN]). Brand-tag slugs and category-hub page_id values must survive cutover without re-slugging — the slug stability guarantee for the migrated vocabulary is a hard contract.
  • 71 distinct tag values and 22 page_id values are currently targeted across live Topic Takeover LIs.
Per-send — what mutates every send
  • Marketing automation clones the template for each dated send and carves a ~5,050-member audience from the parent list.
  • The parent list’s segment then mutates in place so the carve isn’t re-mailed next cycle. The mutation persists; the parent isn’t a stable list at any point in time.
  • Carve lists are never deleted; per-send template clones accumulate cumulatively in Sailthru.
  • Newsletter curation contrast. Custom Content picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot per Topic Takeover send, manual every send. The newsletter is 100% automated. Same content base, two operating models.
Send-fire — the URL handshake
  • No shared registry between Sailthru and GAM. The bridge is the email link.
  • Every link in the email carries a sponsor tag. The reader clicks; GAM reads the sponsor tag from the landing URL and takes over all 7 ad positions on the article page.
  • Competitor exclusion lives inside the GAM ad unit’s targeting expression, not as a Sailthru audience rule, because the bridge is the URL not a shared list.
  • If the URL parameter is wrong, mistyped, or stripped by an upstream link tracker, the takeover silently doesn’t fire and the sponsor sees house ads. Nobody monitors for that failure mode.
Maintenance and reporting — the ongoing tax
  • Steady-state cost: every new sponsor launch makes ad ops hand-type the new sponsor’s newsletter_campaign value into every other live sponsor’s targeting clause — up to ~90 IS_NOT edits per launch (3 tiers × 3 ad units × ~10 concurrent TTs).
  • Visible drift between sponsor exclusion footprints proves the sweep isn’t always done: one sponsor’s tagged LI excludes 60+ historical TTBL values (years of accumulated hygiene debt); another excludes 1. Same calendar month.
  • Off-roster delivery happens: a completed flight in March 2026 delivered 85,736 impressions for a sponsor not on the 15-active-sponsors roster. The roster and the GAM book are not cross-checked.
  • Five of 15 active campaigns exhibit multi-list advertiser mappings — the single-list-per-campaign narrative is approximate.
  • Revenue Ops pulls a monthly impression and click report and forwards to the sponsor.
  • Billing reconciles in the sales system against the original record, not GAM. Canonical revenue per campaign requires a cross-reference.
Where the fragility lives. Single operator on every per-send carve and template edit. External vendor on every GAM ad unit with no internal backup. The in-house S3 ads tool is GenomeWeb-only and undocumented. Competitor exclusion lives inline in GAM targeting rather than as a shared list. The parent audience mutates every send, so no version of any sponsor’s audience is stable at any point in time.

Sources: marketing automation ops audit; GAM line-item targeting export; sales record cross-reference. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: 15 active GenomeWeb sponsor campaigns + ~10 historical Topic Takeover sends (six-send open/CTR mean).

A3Sponsor lifecycle — templated editorial with exclusive sponsorship

Standing theme infrastructure replaces per-campaign builds. Sponsors still pick tags like today; editorial tunes the theme feed per send.

Nothing changes on the outside. Behind the scenes, one audience cohort and one content feed per top-level theme, reused across sponsors. Each send is a light configuration of that standing infrastructure, not a rebuild.

Scope of this diagram. The committed sponsor operating model. Sponsor experience and sales pitch are unchanged. The operational shift — custom build per campaign → standing theme infrastructure edited per send — is scoped in the marketing automation workstream tied to cutover. GAM-side plumbing (YourBow, Convention-2 delivering LIs, competitor IS_NOT edits) is largely preserved.
What changes structurally
  • Theme infrastructure replaces per-sponsor builds. One audience cohort, one content feed, one template per top-level theme. Reused across sponsors. Built once; maintained.
  • Per-send work is a light configuration, not a build. Editorial tunes the theme feed for this sponsor’s tag filter and competitor exclusions. Marketing automation configures date, subject, creative off the theme template. No list carve, no template rebuild, no segment-query mutation.
  • Multiple concurrent sponsors per theme. Exclusive placement per send is preserved via per-send feed-level exclusion. Structural per-package occupancy isn’t required; sponsors share the theme infrastructure across sends.
  • Sailthru side overhead drops substantially. Hand-built custom lists, feeds, and template clones per sponsor retire. Single-operator risk compresses: the work shifts to maintaining theme infrastructure, not rebuilding it per sponsor.
  • GAM side is largely preserved. YourBow still traffics Convention-2 delivering LIs; competitor exclusion via inline IS_NOT edits continues; Elan.Web forward-book auto-sync continues. Ad-ops-side overhead is not resolved by this model — that is a separate workstream.
  • Two bridges to GAM, still coexisting. Page key-values for article-page targeting; newsletter_campaign URL parameter for Topic Takeover boundary-level targeting. Neither replaces the other.
Editorial in the loop per send
  • Editorial tunes the theme feed on every send — set the sponsor tag filter, apply competitor story exclusions. About the same level of judgment as editing a regular editorial newsletter.
  • Custom Content per-send article picks consolidate into the per-send editorial feed edit (or stay as a manual overlay if the team wants). Not a separate workstream.
  • The editorial role this model asks for is ongoing engagement per send, not vocabulary governance alone. Scoping and staffing land in the editorial ops workstream.
What the sponsor still gets — unchanged
  • Same sell-in experience: sponsor picks tags from the library as they do today.
  • Same exclusive placement within each send — no competing sponsors in the same email.
  • Same mutual-exclusivity across concurrent campaigns in the same theme, applied at the feed level per send.
  • Same competitor story exclusion, applied at the feed level per send.
  • Same cadence (multi-send campaign), same deliverables, same verification surface.
  • Mid-flight transition: existing active sponsors continue under current mechanics during the operational switch-over; new sponsors sell into the theme-infrastructure model as it stands up. Cutover timing is a marketing automation ops call.

A4Sponsor open questions

The operating model is set. Open items are scoping detail for the theme infrastructure rollout.

Sponsor-facing product and pitch don’t change. The items below scope the operational build-out tied to cutover.

  • Theme definitions. The top-level themes that carry standing audiences and feeds (Cancer, Diagnostics, Drug Testing, etc.). Derived from how sponsor tag selections actually cluster today. Marketing automation ops + editorial scope the final set and naming.
  • Tag-to-theme mapping for in-flight sponsors. Each active sponsor’s tag list mapped once to the right theme at rollout. Applied without re-running tag selection with the sponsor. Marketing automation ops + sales.
  • In-flight campaign transition. Existing active sponsors continue under current mechanics during the operational switch-over; new sponsors sell into the theme-infrastructure model as it stands up. No grandfathering contortions required at cutover itself.
  • Editorial per-send staffing. The model asks editorial to tune the theme feed on every sponsor send. Roughly editorial-newsletter-level effort per send. Scoping and staffing sit in the editorial ops workstream, not this deck.
  • Custom Content per-send layer disposition. Current per-send Custom Content article picks can fold into the per-send editorial feed edit, or stay as a parallel manual overlay. Editorial + product call.
  • Sponsor perception of topic landing pages. Sponsors today use the topic page as a verification surface. Open: whether the surface needs preservation, can simplify, or migrates to a sponsor-facing dashboard. Ties to the drug-brand-name section question in A10 — if drug/gene-name sections are dropped, sponsors targeting those terms lose their verification page.
  • Ad-ops GAM line-item audit (due 2026-05-01). Could move terms from retireable back to load-bearing. Blocks vocabulary finalization. See A10 Cutover blockers for the full list.
  • GAM-side overhead is not resolved here. YourBow, Convention-2 delivering LIs, and competitor IS_NOT edits continue under this model. A separate ad-ops workstream would be needed to touch GAM-side operator overhead.
  • Competitor-exclusion targeting refactor (ad-ops workstream). Under consideration: replace the cross-sponsor IS_NOT pattern with per-sponsor positive inclusion.
    • Today: newsletter_campaign IS_NOT (sponsor_b_tt, sponsor_c_tt, …) maintained across every LI on every launch.
    • Proposed: each LI holds newsletter_campaign IS <own_sponsor_value> OR NOT SET. No cross-sponsor references. No edits needed when new sponsors launch.
    • Why it’s worth reviewing: eliminates ~900 hand-edits/year floor; dissolves the visible exclusion-list drift problem (one sponsor excludes 60+, another 1) by construction.
    • What the ad-ops team validates: functional equivalence across TTBL / TT / 100% SOV tiers; GAM priority/bid interaction on multi-tag articles; YourBow’s Convention-2 templating fit; migration path (new LIs only vs. retroactive refactor).
    Independent of the Sailthru-side operating model; rides in the ad-ops workstream alongside the May 1 line-item audit.

Bucket 3 · Reader lifecycle

A5Reader lifecycle today

Three opt-in surfaces feed an unbounded follow array, but following a topic does not subscribe to the newsletter — which is why ~12,900 followers translate to ~625 daily sends.

A separate yes/no flag gates the daily send. Send-time match runs inside Sailthru, fully automated.

Three opt-in surfaces, one storage layer
  • All three follow surfaces (preferences UI 85%, topic landing page 9%, article page 6%) write to the same follow array on the Sailthru user record.
  • No auto-assignment. Every follow is a deliberate click.
  • Unbounded means any term in the Drupal vocabulary can be followed — including long-tail terms that appear on a single article. The long tail is the point: niche topics are the product.
  • Average user follow count: 56. Unfollow-to-follow ratio: 1:21 — the array tends to grow.
The newsletter-subscription seam — the structural gap
  • Following a topic does not subscribe the reader to the daily newsletter. Newsletter subscription is a separate action that writes a single yes/no on the user profile.
  • The newsletter audience is gated entirely on that flag. No tag filtering inside the segment itself; matching happens at send time against the follow array.
  • Quantified gap: 12,891 stated followers → 2,181 emailable → 625 active-tier recipients per send.
  • This is the gap to close in the future state — one opt-in that subscribes the reader by definition.
Editorial → content index — the parallel pipeline
  • On every article publish, the editor hand-assigns a primary topic plus secondary topics from the shared Drupal vocabulary.
  • Drupal pushes the article and its tags into the Sailthru content index per save. Push is capped at 15 tags per article. The cap goes away in Arc.
  • The content index is the corpus every sponsor send and every newsletter filter against.
Send-time match — the 100% automated path
  • Each subscriber gets a uniquely assembled email. Sailthru matches content-index articles against the reader’s accumulated tag-interest signal (months of reading history).
  • Newsletter curation contrast. The newsletter is fully automated. Topic Takeover sponsor sends use the same automated tag-filtered feed but layer manual editorial picks (3–5 per slot) on top. Same pipeline, two operating models.
  • The match logic lives only in Sailthru’s UI — not introspectable from outside. Any upstream schema change that breaks the match silently degrades personalization without alerting anyone.
Why on-site doesn’t carry the value. 87.5% of preferences-UI visitors are one-and-done over 90d. Readers configure once and consume via email. The on-site feed is a setup tool, not a destination — which is why the future state retires the topic-page and article-page follow buttons but keeps the preferences UI as the single opt-in surface.

Acquisition is collapsing. New active signups Jan 2026: 11. Feb: 9. Mar: 8. Apr MTD: 2. The product is running on accumulated follow base, not fresh growth.

Sources: Sailthru user records and send logs; on-site preferences-UI analytics; Drupal taxonomy export. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: GenomeWeb stated followers (n = 12,891), emailable (n = 2,181), active-tier recipients/day (n = 625).

A6Reader lifecycle — one opt-in on explicit section follows

Reader picks sections once; that subscribes to the newsletter. Tags stay in the infrastructure layer.

One opt-in, one channel. No inferred reading-signal layer at launch.

What changes for the reader
  • One opt-in surface. Preferences UI stays. On-site topic and article follow buttons retire as separate surfaces; the in-page consumption feed retires.
  • Opt-in equals subscription. The seam between following a section and subscribing to the newsletter closes. No second action required.
  • Followable list is the curated section map. Reader-facing vocabulary tightens from the Drupal long-tail to the editorial section list. Tags don’t appear in the Preferences UI.
  • Same delivery channel, same engagement profile. Newsletter remains the consumption surface; ~3× flagship engagement carries forward.
What tags do instead
  • Advertising targeting. GAM reads tags from page key-values on article ad calls. SOV and Topic Takeover line items target tag-level slugs.
  • Newsletter segmentation infrastructure. Sailthru Interest Lists query against tag-level tokens in segment rules. ~16 active Interest Lists rebuild against slug-form tokens at cutover.
  • Not reader-facing. Tags power the plumbing; the reader picks from sections.
What the Sailthru side looks like
  • Send-time match pattern is unchanged: render against the reader profile, filter the content index, top-N per recipient.
  • Source of the signal changes: page key-values feed the Content Library via scraper (sailthru.tags slug-form, uncapped); the direct push path from Drupal retires.
  • Reader preferences live as explicit section follows on the profile. No Junction inferred-signal layer at launch; Junction’s role is limited to explicit pass-through if the Preference Center UX moves to Arc post-cutover — that’s a separate product call.
Ring-fenced footprint. GNW’s tag set within Arc’s org-global namespace is bounded, curated, and admin-governed on new tag creation. Reversible if the org picks a different direction later.

Architecture reference. Mechanics referenced from Sailthru profile schema, Arc tag-taxonomy permissions model, and GNW taxonomy decision doc. Pulled 04/2026.

Bucket 4 · Editorial & classification

A8Editorial today

Three parallel curation efforts feed one corpus, no shared source of truth, no retirement workflow — drift accumulates.

A per-send manual editorial layer runs on top of the automated feed. No approval step, no retirement workflow.

Three governance lanes, no shared source of truth
  • Editorial leadership maintains a curated My Topics subset (the followable surface readers see).
  • The editorial tagging lead applies tags per article from the broader shared vocabulary, every article, on publish.
  • Ad ops maintains a separate working list of which tags drive sponsor Topic Takeover targeting — a parallel curation effort.
  • The three lists overlap imperfectly. There is no single source of truth for what the product recognizes.
Newsletter curation — explicit contrast
  • Newsletter (My Topics Daily): 100% automated, no editorial curation per send. Sailthru matches content against each reader’s accumulated tag-interest signal.
  • Topic Takeover sponsor sends: automated tag-filtered feed PLUS a manual layer — the Custom Content team picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot, every send.
  • Same content corpus, two operating models. Marketing automation has flagged that nobody can fully explain why the manual layer exists or why Custom Content rather than marketing automation owns it.
What hand-tagging costs — governance drift
  • The per-article time cost is modest (seconds to a minute). The compounding cost is governance drift.
  • 285 of 2,098 free-form topics terms (13.6%) have been used exactly once across six years of content — one-off coinage, entity mentions, or accidental duplicates. Quality of new tags is declining: 53% of 2025-added tags used once; 69% of 2026 YTD.
  • Top 50 tags carry 58% of all assignments. Bottom 40% of the vocabulary (used ≤2 times) carries ~2% of assignments.
  • A meaningful share of terms across the three curated lists have zero load-bearing evidence — no engagement, no follows, no sponsor segment rules. Retireable by definition.
  • Inconsistency across editors is unmeasured but likely material — no approval step, no reconciliation between editors covering similar content.
The long tail is the point. Niche topics with small followings are exactly what the product is for; this isn’t an argument to shrink the vocabulary aggressively. It’s an argument to govern the vocabulary deliberately, not let it accumulate by default. See A9 for the future-state shape.

A9Editorial governs the vocabulary; editors pick from it; admin approval governs new tag creation

Editorial governs sections freely. New tag creation runs through admin approval; governance TBD. Per-article editors freely pick from loaded sections and tags via dropdown.

Two editorial roles, separated
  • Per-article editors tag every article at publish. Free-text creation retires; selection from the dropdown is the mechanism. Per-article time order-of-magnitude unchanged; only net-new-term creation changes.
  • Executive editor governs the curated section map. Adds, retires, merges, aliases as coverage evolves. No admin step on sections.
  • Tag governance (role TBD) reviews additions and retirements to the closed tag vocabulary in scheduled batches. Candidate shapes: executive editor alone; executive editor + product; executive editor + product + ad ops for SOV additions. Decision open; see A10.
Vocabulary provenance
  • Sections from the executive editor’s curated site-structure map (top nav + hamburger menu + hierarchical site tree). Most are load-bearing in six-year Drupal content; a smaller share is zero-evidence (intentional new-for-Arc sections, drug/gene-name placeholders, structural placeholders).
  • Tags from four sources: Topic Takeover campaign tags + My Topics follow terms + 2025 engagement tags + the implicit TT-tier granular terms the executive editor keeps as tags rather than sections. The overwhelming majority are load-bearing in six years of content.
  • Drupal long-tail — the free-text topics tags outside the curated vocabulary — is dropped. Not migrated. Slug stability is preserved for the migrated tags — a hard contract, because live sponsor SOV and Interest List segment rules depend on stable slugs.
How new tag creation works
  • Editors don’t type new tags at publish — selection is the mechanism. Need a new term? File a governance request to the admin role.
  • Enforcement is mechanical, not procedural — new-tag creation is wired to the admin role in Arc at cutover; no workflow discipline to sustain.
  • Admin approval applies to tags, not sections. Editorial retains full section authority.
  • Reversible: if governance shifts later, creation scope can expand. The system starts conservative.
What’s deliberately not here. No AI classifier at launch. No “editorial shifts from tagger to governor” collapse — per-article tagging continues. No cross-brand portability claim — GNW-scoped. No throughput-economics math — there’s no classifier to cost. If classification later becomes worth reconsidering, the decision names four triggers (editorial throughput bottleneck, tag quality degrades, new use cases, cross-brand demand validates) — but that’s a future reopen, not launch scope.

A10Editorial open questions + cutover blockers

Four open editorial calls, one named list of discussion tags, and four cutover blockers that must resolve before vocabulary finalization.

  • Tag-creation admin role. Who holds approval rights on the closed tag vocabulary post-cutover. Candidate shapes: executive editor alone; executive editor + product; executive editor + product + ad ops (for SOV additions). Decision before cutover.
  • Vocabulary governance cadence. Section governance is editorial-internal and ad-hoc. Tag governance runs in scheduled batches. Open: exact cadence (monthly, quarterly, as-needed). Editorial-ops call.
  • Company names in the controlled vocabulary. A meaningful number of company names sit on the boundary — they appear among the most-followed terms and the highest-engagement tags, and sponsor names are used as 100% share-of-voice terms today. Open: whether company names live in the closed vocabulary, a separate entity lane, or both. Editorial leadership’s call.
  • Final vocabulary composition. The earned tag list is committed in composition, not in a specific count — the exact number may shift with drug/gene-name inclusion (below), company-name disposition (above), and the ad-ops GAM audit below.
Named tags that need editorial resolution before finalization
  • Drug-brand-name sections, thin content evidence in Drupal. Krazati, Opdivo, Panitumumab, Enhertu, gefitinib, larotrectinib, Lumakras. These appear in the curated section map but have minimal content evidence today. Resolution: keep as sections and begin tagging to them going forward, or drop. Editorial call.
  • Gene-symbol and diagnostic-technology sections, zero content evidence. BRCA, ALK, BRAF, EGFR, HER2, KRAS, PD-L1, ROS1, MRD testing, CE-IVDR. Same resolution question as above.
  • Normalization mismatches — concept exists, tag form differs. PrecisionOncology (Drupal has “Testing Strategies for Precision Oncology”); COVID (My Topics slug; Drupal uses “COVID-19”); Mass Spec (My Topics; Drupal uses “Mass Spectrometry”); AutoimmuneDisease (curated; Drupal uses “Autoimmune Disease Research News”).   • Drupal long-tail disposition — decided. The free-text topics tags outside the curated vocabulary are dropped at cutover, not migrated. This is a decision item, not an open question. Slug stability is preserved for the migrated vocabulary.
