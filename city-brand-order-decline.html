00Order decline data review

What the data says about the City Brand order decline.

The data shows the same pattern across AN, MH, and AA.

  • The decline in orders is real.
  • City brand orders down −53% to −59% vs a late 2025 baseline, which includes pre-Arc decline trend.
  • Following Arc migration, daily order rate dipped ~20% across all city brands in aggregate.
  • Across traffic, publishing, paywall exposure, and tech migrations, the data shows only two cross-brand signals aligned with order decline — the intro-rate acquisition program and weekly publishing volume.

  • Customer-LTV (table 1): each customer attributed to their FIRST paid-tier entry in the window. LTV = Σ(amount − amount_refunded) for paid=TRUE payments across ALL of that customer's subscriptions, any brand, any tier. "Took another paid sub" counts any later non-is_comp individual sub at a different price or interval. "$300+ LTV" is a rough "real subscriber" threshold.
  • Era economics (table 2): M6-observable gross payments per acquired sub, aggregated to era totals (Phase-9 methodology, unchanged). M6 is the latest horizon fully observable for the heavy-discount cohorts.
  • Sub-level vs. customer-level: an earlier version of this slide reported "$0.50 M12 rev / sub, $1,396 program lifetime revenue" for the $1 tier. That figure counts only revenue from the $1 subscription records themselves. The current table reports lifetime revenue per customer across all of their subscriptions, so downstream conversion (22% of $1 customers took another paid sub; 8% reached $300+ LTV) is included. Both views are reported for transparency.
  • Data drift vs. Phase 9 (2026-04-16): $1 cohort now 2,841 subs / $1,402 sub-level revenue (Phase 9: 2,816 / $1,396). Era totals match within 1%.

Data & methodology

  • Sources: Orders from cdw_02_stg.pelcro_subscriptions. Articles from cdw_01_raw.drupal_articles (pre-migration) and cdw_02_stg.arc_articles (post-migration). Redshift, pulled 2026-04-18.
  • Scope: 8 brands — CCB, CDB, CCL, CNY, CGR (city), AA, ANG, MHC (comparable Arc-migrated).
  • Window: 2024-06-01 through 2026-04-16 (22 months, ~98 weeks per brand). Selected for statistical power and to include AN's full pre/post-Arc era.
  • Orders universe: is_individual=TRUE, COALESCE(is_comp,FALSE)=FALSE, source IN ('Web','Website','website','web'). Web-only filter is required — without it, 52% of rows are bulk import migrations and 15% are comp, which would drown out organic acquisition signal.
  • Order anomaly exclusions: Same operational cohorts as A8 — CCB Oct-2024 $18/mo migration spike; CCB Nov-2025 $20/mo mass-promo cancel batch; CCL $0.01 backfill.
  • Articles — source reconciliation: For each brand, drupal_articles used for dates before that brand's Arc migration date; arc_articles used for dates on or after. This avoids the ~2-month double-count on MHC (Drupal and Arc ran in parallel through mid-2025) and smaller overlaps on AN and AA. Brand-code remapping: content-table "MH" maps to Pelcro "MHC"; "AN" maps to "ANG".
  • Content filter: AA content_type='Work' (Drupal) and subtype='work' (Arc) excluded. This content is ad-campaign showcases published on behalf of clients, not reported editorial. Verified against the content KPI tables: in Drupal era, Work was 20% of AA's article count but drove 0.08% of total order finish-assists; in Arc era, Work was 11% of articles but drove 3.8% of finish-assists. Per-visit conversion rate 31%/47% of Article rate. 1,005 AA records excluded (587 Drupal + 418 Arc). No equivalent content category exists on other brands.
  • Aggregation: Weekly, using date_trunc('week', d)::date (Monday-start). Per-brand effective window constrained to the range where article data exists for that brand (CGR Arc-only, 4 weeks).
  • Pearson r: Computed manually (Redshift has no CORR function) with float casts to prevent integer overflow in SUM-of-squares.
  • Detrending: 8-week centered rolling-mean residuals on both series. Published values minus 8-week rolling mean (4 weeks each side where available). 4-week also computed as a robustness check.
  • Tests run:
    1. Per-brand raw weekly r over full window.
    2. Per-brand cleaned weekly r (AA Work filter applied).
    3. Per-brand detrended r (8-week rolling residuals).
    4. Per-brand lagged r at 1, 2, 4, 8 weeks (publishing leading orders).
    5. Portfolio aggregate (all 8 brands summed per week): raw, cleaned, detrended, monthly.
    6. Cross-brand Spearman rank correlation on % change pre-shutoff (2025-04-01 to 2025-07-31) vs post-shutoff (2025-12-01 to 2026-03-31).
    7. Pre/post-Arc shock (90-day windows) for AN, AA, MHC — the three brands with enough post-Arc history to test.
  • Normal-weeks filter: The reported headline portfolio numbers exclude 7 non-representative weeks to keep the comparison on routine business activity. All weeks remain plotted in the chart; the footnote in Section 03 makes this explicit. Excluded weeks:
    • 2024-11-25, 2025-11-24 — Thanksgiving week (4-day US holiday schedule depresses both article and order activity).
    • 2024-12-23, 2024-12-30, 2025-12-22, 2025-12-29 — Christmas and New Year weeks (widely depressed activity).
    • 2026-01-05 — last-call rush. Orders spiked to 1,235 as subscribers grabbed the intro plans before the 01/15 shutoff. Not representative of underlying demand.
    Both all-weeks and normal-weeks figures are computed for transparency (all weeks: r = +0.71 weekly, +0.79 detrended, +0.89 monthly; normal weeks: r = +0.65 weekly, +0.69 detrended, +0.91 monthly). The framing in Sections 00 and 03 uses normal weeks as the headline because the masking narrative (indexed windows) requires steady-state comparisons.
  • What the data supports: Week-to-week co-movement between publishing volume and paid-order volume. Signal is positive on every brand tested, strengthens after detrending (ruling out shared-trend artifact), and is very strong at the portfolio level (r = +0.65 weekly cleaned, +0.69 detrended, +0.91 monthly, normal weeks). Indexed window comparison (Section 03) shows orders held near baseline during the intro era while articles had already declined ~15%-18%; post-shutoff, orders landed ~6 points below articles at 69 / 75 of baseline — beneath the pre-intro line.
  • Discrete event vs longer horizon: At the 01/15/2026 shutoff specifically, weekly orders dropped −19% and weekly articles dropped only −3% in the matched pre/post window (2025-12 to 2026-01-12 vs 2026-01-19 to 2026-02-23, normal weeks only). The cliff itself is mechanical — removing plans does not affect editorial cadence. Over longer horizons the series co-move. The two observations are compatible because the correlation captures week-to-week rhythm, not event-driven shocks to the order side.
  • What the data does not support: A causal claim that publishing volume change drives order change. Three reasons:
    1. Lagged correlations are weak and inconsistent across brands. Concurrent r dominates. If publishing drove orders, we would expect a peak at lag +1 to +4 weeks; instead peak is at lag 0.
    2. Cross-brand rank test on pre-shutoff vs post-shutoff % change: Spearman ρ = +0.25. Brands with bigger publishing drops tend to have bigger order drops, but modestly.
    3. Per-brand pre/post-Arc shock test (90-day windows): all three testable brands directionally disagree with the hypothesis. MHC articles −26% / orders +16%. ANG articles +11% / orders −4%. AA articles −1% / orders −19%.
    The signal is consistent with both series responding to shared portfolio-level forces (seasonal audience cycles, macro news flow) rather than publishing directly driving subscription behavior.
  • Prior exploratory run: An earlier exploratory correlation produced lower per-brand r values (e.g., ANG +0.24 vs this run's +0.44). Root cause identified: prior run used naïve UNION of drupal_articles and arc_articles without per-brand migration-date deduplication, which double-counted the Drupal/Arc overlap during each brand's migration window. This read uses strict migration-date cutoff; the higher r values reflect elimination of overlap noise, not a methodological divergence in the other direction.
  • Reproducibility: Script and data at a8-correlation-analysis-2026-04-18/ in the repo root. analyze.py runs the per-brand pipeline from the saved JSON queries. aggregate.py produces the all-weeks portfolio figures. normal-weeks-only.py produces the normal-weeks portfolio figures and the indexed-window table used in Section 03.

A9Scope, limitations, open phases

What this read can and can't carry.

  • AA Arc confound. Arc launch and intro rate authorization are two days apart (04/07/2025 vs 04/09/2025). April 2025 effects on AA cannot be cleanly separated into Arc attributable vs intro attributable. AN is used as the isolation case; AA carries the confound caveat.
  • Arc migration SEO cost on city brands. Separate from the order volume question. Organic sessions −26% to −37% and social −37% to −58% on city brands in Window C. A reindexing signature consistent with a site migration.
  • Sessions vs. unique visitors. Phase 2 used sessions per brand. Adversarial validation pulled UVs for direct reach and found it rose on four of five city brands B→C. Organic and social UVs have not been pulled yet. Before extending an SEO framing to AN and AA, UV-by-channel should be pulled for those brands.
  • What hasn't been tested. Paid-marketing spend alignment to the event windows (Phase 7). Qualitative confirmation from the subscriptions team on the Dec 2025 shutoff intent (Phase 8). Sailthru deliverability and list-health (Phase 6). Coupon-intro cohort retention separate from the $1-plan retention already covered in A1.
  • Pre-2024 baseline. This read uses May 2024 onward. 2023 data wasn't pulled. If 2023 rates were materially different, the pre-intro baseline may shift.
